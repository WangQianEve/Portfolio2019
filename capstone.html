Summary
Problem Framing
Re-design Liquidnet’s alert system to encourage users to fully utilize the advanced features.
This project is sponsored by Liquidnet, a company that provides cutting-edge financial technology to institutional traders and asset managers. The challenge is that users often only use Liquidnet’s basic trading features, and even minimize the interface most of the time. This way, the advanced technologies that can identify new trading opportunites are not fully utilized, thus causing less trades than expected within Liquidnet’s network.
Solution
A comprehensive set of alert design guidelines, along with validated prototypes as proof of concept.

Our designs are focused around these 4 aspects:

  • Alerts that manage users' attention through color and sound.
  • Alerts that convince users to take action with a right interactive information hierarchy.
  • Alerts that bring easy customization to users, and bring data to the AI system.
  • Alerts that works well in different contexts and workflows.

Example guidelines and prototypes
Salient color is most effective for attracting attention at a trading desk.
We tested the effects of different combinations of color, animation, and sound at a simulated trading desk. We discovered that salient color works the best because of higher contrast. So in our prototype, we used color to show different levels of priority.
The right information hierarchy and interactions helps initiate actions.
Unlike the commonly used “call to action” design, traders don’t want to see the action immediately because their decision making process needs more evidence. We used visual hierarchy and progressive disclosure interaction to fit to their thinking process. We also utilized techniques to gain more user trust for the recommendations.
Easier customization helps build a more personalized system.
Previously, Liquidnet’s customization settings are in a separate page, and users hardly use them. To bring the settings more accessible, we designed several in-context customization methods. The data generated by these interactions can also be used to build more personalized trading recommendations.
Supporting alert usage in key workflows brings value.
Through user research, we identified several key workflows related to alerts, like quickly accessing specific analytics, building a report to share, and sharing alert configurations. We built corresponding protoypes as shown on the right, and tested them.

User Research

Before the project scope was narrowed down to alerts, we did user research to learn about the problem space in general. This section talks about our research methods, challenges, and findings.

Jump to design research if you are more interested in our journey around alerts.

Background Research
To quickly get onboarded with this unfamiliar domain, we utilized a wide range of resources.

To quickly get onboarded, we started with talking to friends who has relevant background, online videos introducing trading knowledge (Youtube, Investopedia, etc.), movies and documentaries talking about traders, even games simulating trading experience. This allowed us to study professional materials provided by our client.

resources we looked into
products we examined
To learn about domain-specific design paradigms, we researched on existing products.

We first went through Liquidnet’s product demo and conducted heuristic evaluation. Then we examined competitors’ products to understand the landscape and design patterns in the field.

Stakeholder Interview
With no direct access to our target users, we interviewed 22 stakeholder and proxy users.

Because we have no access to target users, we instead interviewed different stakeholders and proxy users from client-side and our own network. A technique we used with extremely busy interviewees is to bring pre-made artifacts like customer journey map for interviewees to correct. This way we can make the most of our limited time.

me interviewing a stakeholder
Through the interviews, we gained a basic understanding of a trader’s life and needs.

We learned about trader’s main stakeholders, the interaction with them, trader’s daily workflow and digital workspace. We also used affinity mapping to synthesize our notes, and identified 6 major user needs:

  1. Need for customization because traders’ workflow varies a lot
  2. Need for efficient data consumption
  3. Need for design supporting collaboration
  4. Need for closure to learn from failure
  5. Need for being innovative
  6. Need for understanding theories behind tools
Analogous Domain Research
To understand traders’ mindset and emotions, we conducted contextual interviews with online poker players.

We discovered that online poker is a very similar domain to trading, and is even often used to train young traders. So we did several contextual interviews to understand their mindset and emotional needs. We reviewed our observations on a matrix, which gave us inspirations for potential solutions to emotional problems of traders. One major insight we got is "Positive reinforcement helps maintain a healthy and rational trading mindset".

Shadowing / Observation
We gained a more comprehensive understanding of their needs through observing their daily workflow.

We observed the trading desk in our client’s company during market opening, mid-day, and market closing time. While observing, we used AEIOU framework to focus around activities (A), events (E), interactions (I), objects (O), and users (U).

Key Insights from Shadowing

Market closing is the most stressful time

Among these 3 time periods, closing time is the busiest and most stressful, during which traders collaborate more.

Different preferences for sounds

Not all traders use sounds, and each has different preferences for sounds.

Report building is repetitive work

Traders communicate with portfolio managers and clients using reports, which requires considerable manual work.

Scroll-and-click can be cumbersome

Sometimes, when navigating through lots of information, scrolling can be too slow, and even cumbersome.

Design Research

After having a holistic understanding of the domain, users, and product, our project scope was narrowed down by the client to be alert system design guidelines.

This section talks about our how we generated a set of design guidelines based on analogous domain research and “research through design” method.

Analogous Domain Research
We learned about general design principles for alert systems through literature review and case studies.

Our research was focused around 3 topics: attracting attention, guiding action, and gaining user trust. After brainstorming, we investigated various domains including retail, advertisement, emergencies, gaming, notifications, autonomous driving, etc.

See on the right our major findings.

Experience Prototyping
In our only chance with target users, we chose "research through design" method.

We only had one chance to talk with our target users in person. This led us to use the “research through design” approach rather than exploratory research, so that we can gain a more precise understanding of their needs and preferences.

Prototypes were built based our previous research and questions we want answers for.

We rapidly built prototypes based on previous user research and design research. Then we tested traders’ reaction to our concepts in a simulated trading environment. This allowed us to generate tailored design principles in the future.

See on the right the settings and prototypes we tested.

Feedback Grid
We synthesized our notes and generated design guidelines tailored for trading alert system.

For synthesizing, we used the feedback grid method, and modified the 4th quadrant to better fit our research. Our feedback grid has four quadrants: “what worked”, “what should be changed”, “new ideas”, and “general behaviors”. This helped us easily generate design guidelines from each quadrants. For instance, from quadrant 1 and 2, we concluded DOs and DON’Ts, from quadrant 3, we extracted design limits/preferences.

us working on feedback grid
Example Guidelines

A mini window can be useful

Users preferred the un-intrusive feeling and space-saving characteristics of the “Bubble” prototype, though the visual design is too different from the current Liquidnet branding.

Salient color is the most noticeable

Salient color is the most noticeable, and it’s easy to read. However, care should be exercised to avoid unintentional attention (e.g., when a less important uncolored alert comes in, the colored alerts should not move).

Hierarchical views are important

Traders are faced with an overwhelming amount of information. A hierarchical view that displays details and quick actions on hover can be less obstrusive.

Customized grouping is needed

Traders want to group alerts differently based on their current workflow and preferences.

Design

Our next step is to build prototypes that apply the principles we concluded. This section talks about our ideation, prototyping, and iterating process.

Brainstorming
Based on key guidelines, we brainstormed new ideas after reviewed all the previous research findings...

We used “walking the wall” method, which means putting up all previous research findings on the wall and reviewing them to generate new ideas. Our ideas were focused around visual hierarchy and customization. We also have two other in-context features: report building and type-to-navigate.

"walking the wall" and brainstorm
some of our storyboards
Storyboarding
... and sketched our ideas into user stories to validate and refine them.

Then we separately sketched our ideas for each focus and compiled them into a user story. Through an internal critique, we integrated our parallel designs into one set. Storyboarding not only helped us validate the ideas in the context, but also helped everyone in the team be on the same page.

Iterating
Embodiment, testing, and iterating.

Ideas were then embodied into digital prototypes. We tested the prototypes with CMU business school students, Liquidnet users from our own network, and relationship managers from Liquidnet.

Reflection

This is a special project for me, because lots of challenges we encountered was my first time. I learned a lot, and I feel proud of how we handled them! For instance:

  • I learned how to quickly learn about an unfamiliar domain, how to conduct user research with limited user/limited time, and how to proceed when there’s no clear direction.
  • I learned how to try out new research methods under different conditions/with different stakeholders. And after the first trial, I know better how to conduct that method.
  • I learned how to talk about UX with different people.

Another big takeaway is, through the journey, I learned how to form a wonderful team with people from totally different backgrounds and mindsets, and became friends!
