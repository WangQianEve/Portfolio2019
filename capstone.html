Summary
Who’s the client?
Liquidnet is a global institutional investment network that provides cutting-edge financial technology like trading algorithms, trading analytics, trading recommendations etc. to its users, who are mainly institutional traders and asset managers.
Project Prompt
Re-design Liquidnet's alert system, which provides individualized actionable alerts to traders, to increase product usage. And conclude generalizable design guidelines for future design of the alert system.
Problem Framing
We broke down the project prompt into 3 research focuses :
  • How might we manage attention grabbing at a busy trading desk?
  • How might we guide the user to take action on the alerts?
  • How might we get users’ trust for the actions the system suggests?
Solution
A comprehensive alert design guidelines with supporting research and validated prototypes as proof of concepts. The guidelines are focused around attention management (through color, sound, and animation), guiding action (through visual hierarchy), gaining trust for an intelligent system (through easy customization and navigation), and other user needs we found critical through research (like report building).
View Design Guidelines
Example guidelines and prototypes
Mainly use the combination of color and movement to attract different levels of attention.
We tested the effects of different color, animation, and sound with traders at a simulated trading desk. And decided to mainly use colors to address priority.
Use clear visual hierarchy and interaction design to guide action.
Decision making for traders is more complicated than common consumers. So, when an alert comes in, traders need to first quickly understand what happened, then be convinced by the reasoning, and lastly, take action. We used visual hierarchy and progressive disclosure interaction to fit this decision making process.
Increase trust for personalized alerts using branding, social impact, and easy customization.
Liquidnet uses machine learning technology to provide personalized trading recommendations. To increase users’ trust on it, we branded them visually, included other traders’ behavior to build credibility, and designed easy control for customization.
Support minimized view, alert sharing, and information navigation.
3 other alert-related user needs are revealed through our user testing: need to minimize the front-end while not missing anything, need to share information and configuration with teammates, need to quickly pull relevant alerts and analytics. So we included 3 new features into the alert stream to address these needs.

User Research
Background Research
To quickly learn about an unfamiliar domain, use casual methods before formal research.

To quickly get onboard, we started with talking to friends who has relevant background, and also online videos introducing trading knowledge on Youtube and Investopedia, movies and documentaries talking about traders, even played games simulating the experience.

resources we looked into
Learn about design patterns from competitors

Afterwards, we went through materials our client provided, and looked at their competitors’ webstie to know about the landscape and design patterns in the field.

Stakeholder Interview
Talk with 22 stakeholders to learn about target users.

Because we have no access to target users, we instead interviewed stakeholders from client-side and proxy users (other types of traders) from our own network.

One technique we used a lot is to bring pre-made artifacts like customer journey map for interviewees to correct, this way we can make the most of our limited time.

Me interviewing stakeholder
Contextual interview (Online Poker)
Positive reinforcement helps maintain a healthy and rational trading mindset.

We discovered that online poker is a very similar domain to trading, and is even often used to train young traders. So we did several contextual interviews to understand their mindset and emotional needs. After interviewing, we plotted our observations on a matrix, and reviewed the reason and resolution of poker players for the bad emotional situations.

emotion map
Shadowing
First hand research: A more vivid and comprehensive understanding of traders’ life and needs.

We observed the trading desk at our client’s company at market opening, mid-day, and market closing time. When observing, we used AEIOU framework to guide us and remind ourselves not to forget certain aspects. AEIOU represent activities (A), events (E), interactions (I), objects (O), and users (U).

Next Steps
The current solution still has flaws. Future steps for this project can be:
  • Test with more users who have visual impairments to refine current design and scripts.
  • Design a personality for the skill to make it more adorable and memorable.
  • Identify and design for higher-level user needs like emotional need etc.
  • Explore potential ways to incorporate/tackle more high-value-high-cost features.
  • Expand target user group and design for the general crowd.
